A Case Study of
NAE’s Work in  

Texas

Introduction

Immigration is under threat worldwide. In America, Texas is ground zero. There are anti-immigrant bills in the legislature, families fleeing violence arriving by the thousands at the border, and acute shortages of workers across almost every industry in the state. There's an imperative to fix the system and a real risk of backsliding.
‍
This is a hard issue, and finding solutions is going to require a novel approach that combines the right message and the right messengers in the right places to fight back. It will require making the case that immigration matters to every Texan and to each of our local communities. And it will require advocacy that directly address the reasons that many Americans are anxious about immigration.

2001 Dream Act

Texas became the first state in the nation to pass legislation expanding access to in-state tuition to all eligible students, regardless of immigration status.  For nearly two decades this law, championed by then-Governor Rick Perry, has helped young Texans get the education they need to fulfill their own potential and help the state’s economy thrive.

Efforts to Repeal the Dream Act

Following a national wave of anti-immigrant state legislation in 2011, there have been attempts to repeal the Texas Dream Act in every legislative session since 2013, a move that would. cost the Texas economy nearly $400 million in lost economic activity each year.

Bringing Business to the Table

In 2017, the Texas business community mobilized to fight the discriminatory anti-LGBTQ  Texas “Bathroom Bill,” forming a first-of-its-kind coalition where businesses, big and small, came together to fight the bill on the basis of the economic impact to the state.The success of the business community effectively killing a priority of both the Governor and Lt. Governor paved the way for Texas businesses to challenge the legislature on other discriminatory policies that would tarnish Texas’ reputation as being a welcoming state, including fighting anti-immigrant legislation and successfully protecting in-state tuition for all Texas students.

The Problem

Texas has the second-longest international border, the second-most representation in the House of Representatives, and the second-largest amount of immigrant residents, however, Texans have become increasingly concerned they have the most to gain or the most to lose economically when it comes to immigration policies.

With changing state-level leadership and federal immigration reform at a standstill, Texas was reaching a tipping point that was being pushed by a series of efforts to repeal in-state tuition for immigrant students and the passage of anti-sanctuary cities legislation in 2017 that took the business community by surprise. Texas needed data to demonstrate immigrants’ positive contributions to the state’s economy, and that data needed a heartbeat.

Texas Compact

On February 26, NAE launched Texans for Economic Growth, a coalition of more than 60 prominent Texas business leaders and associations across industries and across the state dedicated to recognizing and supporting the positive impact immigrants have on the Texas economy.

With its launch, the coalition released the Texas Compact on Immigration, a set of three principles signed by 85 Texas businesses and industry leaders, including 21 chambers of commerce, calling for smart immigration policies at the federal and state levels, including protecting the Texas Dream Act.

Informing the Conversation with Data

In March, New American Economy launched the latest version of Map the Impact, an interactive map that quantifies immigrant contributions at the national, state, city, and congressional district levels, and across industries. The findings made a splash in Texas, including coverage in Austin NPR, Dallas Radio, and The Dallas Morning News.

In conjunction with the launch of Texans for Economic Growth, we released a report analyzing that it will cost Texas $400 million if in-state tuition for Dreamers is repealed. The potential economic loss to the state resonated with Texans, with business leaders and Dreamers alike advocating for protecting the law, from Fort Bend to Austin and beyond.

Telling stories that resonate

We've continued to build momentum in the months since the launch of Texas for Economic Growth, with coverage of Texans for Economic Growth in the Dallas Morning News, and OpEds from the Austin Statesman Editorial Board as well as opeds from Dreamers who would be affected by the tuition access repeal in the Houston Chronicle, San Antonio Express-News, and the Austin American-Statesman.

On March 7, NAE co-sponsored The Future of Immigration in Houston, a half-day conference that brought together business and civic voices focused on maximizing the economic potential of Houston’s immigrant residents and changing the narrative around immigration.

One month after Texans for Economic Growth launched, NAE participated in a special event, Rational Middle: Immigration at Bullock Texas State History Museum. Byron Cook, former Chairman of Texas House State Affairs Committee and honorary Texans for Economic Growth member explained “We can cuss and discuss, but it’s the folks in Washington that have to have the will [for immigration reform].”

Reaching New Audiences

NAE also used storytelling around the arts and food to show the impact that immigrants have in shaping Texas’ culture. In the process, we reached new audiences and reached readers who are not as politically engaged or active.

And NAE’s’ journalist team secured an op-ed that spoke to Americans who value the contributions of the armed forces:
Many of the soldiers securing our borders are immigrants who are proud to defend the U.S.The Dallas Morning NewsMarch 19, 2019

What’s Next?

These efforts in Texas have ignited interest for more opportunities to engage leaders on immigration across the state. NAE will continue to share new research and discuss tools Texans can use to change the narrative on immigration at events like the Texas Chambers of Commerce Executives conference and the North Texas Commission’s immigration luncheon this summer.

The impact is spreading. Inspired by the Houston immigration conference, Rice University’s Glasscock School of Continuing Studies will now offer a short course on “Universal City: Immigration and the Future of Houston and the U.S.”  next fall. NAE will co-teach a session on comprehensive immigration policies in November.

Furthermore, more rural and conservative communities like Lubbock, Longview, and Waco are now engaging in the discussion and exploring opportunities to host events, community ambassador trainings, and share their NAE reports publicly.
